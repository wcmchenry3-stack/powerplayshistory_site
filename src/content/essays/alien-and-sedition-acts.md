# The Alien and Sedition Acts: Fear as Policy, Power as Habit

## **I. The Quasi-War: Crisis Without Declaration**

By 1798, the United States was barely a decade into its constitutional experiment. The Constitution had been ratified in 1788; George Washington had left office only the year before; and the machinery of federal authority was still new, fragile, and contested.[^1]

American merchant ships were being seized by French privateers. Cargo was confiscated. Sailors were detained. What followed was not a declared war—but it was real fighting at sea.[^2] Historians call it the *Quasi-War*, an undeclared naval conflict between the United States and France fought from 1798 to 1800.[^3]

Congress authorized a naval buildup. The army was expanded. George Washington was called out of retirement to command a provisional army.[^4] The republic braced for war—without formally declaring one.

President John Adams walked a narrow line. He strengthened defenses and expanded the navy, yet resisted mounting pressure from Federalist war hawks who sought a full-scale declaration against France.[^5] As Alexander DeConde notes, Adams believed war would threaten the survival of the young republic more than protect it.[^6]

The fear, however, did not stay at sea.

---

## **II. From External Threat to Internal Suspicion**

The late 1790s were saturated with suspicion. Federalists believed the United States faced not merely a foreign adversary but internal subversion. The French Revolution had radicalized European politics. The word “Jacobin” became shorthand in America for dangerous democratic extremism.[^7]

Stanley Elkins and Eric McKitrick describe the period as one in which Federalists saw “organized opposition” not as normal party politics but as destabilizing factionalism that could destroy the republic.[^8]

And who, in Federalist eyes, seemed most likely to harbor French sympathies?

Recent immigrants—particularly French refugees and Irish arrivals—many of whom leaned toward Thomas Jefferson’s Democratic-Republican opposition.[^9]

The crisis abroad thus merged with a crisis of belonging at home.

---

## **III. The Four Acts: A System of Levers**

In the summer of 1798, Congress passed four statutes collectively known as the Alien and Sedition Acts.[^10] They were not isolated measures. Together, they formed a coherent framework aimed at political stability as Federalists defined it.

### **1\. The Naturalization Act (June 18, 1798\)**

The law extended the residency requirement for citizenship from five years to fourteen.[^11] It also lengthened the required declaration of intent period from three years to five.[^12]

This was not administrative tinkering. As James Morton Smith explains, Federalists believed that recent immigrants disproportionately supported the Republican opposition, and extending the timeline for naturalization would delay their political influence.[^13]

Citizenship was voting power. Delaying citizenship delayed the electorate’s transformation.

---

### **2\. The Alien Friends Act (June 25, 1798\)**

The Alien Friends Act authorized the President to order the removal of any non-citizen he deemed “dangerous to the peace and safety of the United States,” or suspected of “treasonable or secret machinations.”[^14]

Notably, no criminal conviction was required. The power rested on executive judgment alone.

James Morton Smith emphasizes that although Adams never formally deported anyone under the Act, its existence served as a powerful instrument of intimidation and political pressure.[^15]

The threat itself altered behavior.

---

### **3\. The Sedition Act (July 14, 1798\)**

The Sedition Act made it a federal crime to “print, utter or publish… any false, scandalous and malicious writing” against the government, Congress, or the President, with intent to bring them into “contempt or disrepute.”[^16]

Conspicuously, the Vice President was not included among the protected officials—a detail that mattered because Thomas Jefferson, the Vice President, was Adams’s chief political rival.[^17]

At least seventeen indictments were issued under sedition enforcement (fourteen under the Act itself and three under common law).[^18] Ten resulted in convictions.[^19]

The prosecutions overwhelmingly targeted Republican editors and activists.[^20]

---

### **4\. The Alien Enemies Act (July 6, 1798\)**

Unlike the other three laws, the Alien Enemies Act applied only in times of declared war. It authorized the President, upon proclamation, to detain, restrain, or remove nationals of a hostile foreign power residing in the United States.[^21]

Crucially, it had no expiration clause.

Three of the four Acts would lapse or be repealed within a few years. The Alien Enemies Act remained.

---

# **IV. The Public Face of Enforcement: Lyon, Bache, Callender**

The Sedition Act did not sit quietly on the books.

It was used.

And its earliest targets were not obscure figures — they were prominent Republican voices.

## **Matthew Lyon: A Congressman in Jail**

Matthew Lyon was a sitting Democratic-Republican congressman from Vermont.[^22]

In 1798, he published a letter accusing President Adams of “ridiculous pomp” and an “unbounded thirst for ridiculous pomp, foolish adulation, and selfish avarice.”[^23]

The language was sharp. It was partisan. It was meant to wound politically.

Under the Sedition Act, Lyon was indicted, convicted, fined $1,000, and sentenced to four months in jail.[^24]

That fine was not incidental. It was among the largest imposed under the Act.[^25]

And here is the moment that made Lyon’s case politically explosive:

He was reelected to Congress while serving his sentence.[^26]

James Morton Smith notes that Lyon’s imprisonment transformed him into a symbol of resistance.[^27] Republicans portrayed the prosecution not as law enforcement, but as an attempt to silence elected opposition.

The spectacle mattered more than the sentence.

A member of Congress was jailed under a federal speech law passed by the party controlling Congress.

That image traveled.

---

## **Benjamin Franklin Bache: The Editor Who Never Made It to Trial**

Benjamin Franklin Bache, grandson of Benjamin Franklin, was editor of the *Aurora*, one of the most influential Republican newspapers in the country.[^28]

The *Aurora* regularly attacked Federalist policies and the Adams administration.

Bache was indicted under the Sedition Act in 1798.[^29]

He did not live to stand trial.

He died of yellow fever before the case proceeded.[^30]

But his indictment itself sent a message: the federal government was willing to prosecute the leading opposition newspaper editor.

Smith emphasizes that the prosecutions under the Act overwhelmingly targeted Republican editors.[^31] Wendell Bird’s archival research confirms that sedition indictments were concentrated on opposition press figures rather than Federalist critics.[^32]

The chilling effect did not require conviction.

Indictment was enough to demonstrate risk.

---

## **James Thomson Callender: Partisan Firebrand**

James Thomson Callender was perhaps the most inflammatory of the prosecuted writers.

A Scottish-born journalist, Callender published attacks describing Adams as corrupt, monarchical, and unfit for office.[^33]

He was indicted, tried, convicted, fined $200, and sentenced to nine months in prison.[^34]

Unlike Lyon, Callender was not an elected official. Unlike Bache, he did not die before trial.

His case illustrates how aggressively the Sedition Act could be applied to partisan journalism.

Smith describes Callender as one of the clearest examples of the Act’s use against ideological opponents rather than neutral law enforcement.[^35]

Callender would later turn against Jefferson — a reminder that the press ecosystem of the 1790s was volatile, personal, and ruthless.

But under Adams, it was federal prosecution that elevated him.

---

# **V. John Daly Burk: Where Sedition Meets Alienage**

John Daly Burk complicates the story because he sits at the intersection of two Acts.

Burk was an Irish immigrant, playwright, and Republican writer.[^36]

He mocked Federalist leaders in theatrical and journalistic form.[^37]

But unlike Lyon or Callender, Burk was not a citizen.

That mattered.

He was indicted under the Sedition Act.[^38] But because he was a non-citizen, he also fell within the reach of the Alien Friends Act — which authorized the President to order the removal of any alien deemed dangerous to the peace and safety of the United States.[^39]

Burk negotiated.

He reportedly agreed to leave the country if prosecution were dropped.[^40]

The government dismissed the charges.

But Burk did not actually depart the United States.

Instead, he went into hiding in Virginia, reemerging after the expiration of the Alien Friends Act.[^41]

James Morton Smith is clear: no formal deportation order was executed under the Alien Friends Act.[^42]

Yet Burk’s case demonstrates that the statute functioned as leverage. It did not need to produce mass removals to reshape behavior.

The line between citizen and non-citizen became a political vulnerability.

And in moments of crisis, vulnerability can be powerfully coercive.

---

# **IV. Thomas Cooper: When Criticism Became Criminal**

Thomas Cooper was not an obscure agitator. He was a lawyer, scientist, political thinker, and recent immigrant from England.[^43] In 1799, he published a handbill criticizing President Adams, accusing him of increasing taxes, expanding executive power, and mishandling foreign policy.[^44]

These were political arguments — pointed, partisan, but grounded in public policy.

Under the Sedition Act, however, criticism could be prosecuted if it was deemed “false, scandalous and malicious” and intended to bring the President into contempt.[^45] The statute allowed truth as a defense — a significant departure from English common law — but required the defendant to prove it.[^46]

Cooper was indicted in April 1800\.

His trial took place before Supreme Court Justice Samuel Chase, who was riding circuit in Pennsylvania.[^47] Chase had already gained a reputation among Republicans for his combative courtroom demeanor and Federalist sympathies.[^48]

At trial, Cooper attempted to demonstrate that his statements about Adams were factually grounded. He introduced public documents and defended his reasoning.[^49] The prosecution argued not simply that the statements were inaccurate, but that they were calculated to inflame public opinion and undermine government authority.[^50]

He was convicted.

The sentence: six months’ imprisonment and a fine of $400.[^51]

To modern ears, $400 may not sound severe. But economic historian John J. McCusker’s long-range price index demonstrates that late eighteenth-century dollar values cannot be casually compared to modern currency.[^52] In 1799, $400 represented a substantial financial burden — particularly when combined with months of lost income during incarceration.[^53]

Smith notes that fines under the Sedition Act were deliberately punitive, not symbolic.[^54]

Cooper’s conviction illustrates something essential about the Act’s operation: it criminalized not merely falsehood, but political hostility framed as malicious intent. The courtroom became an arena in which partisan argument could be reinterpreted as criminal agitation.

---

# **V. David Brown and the Liberty Pole: Revolutionary Language Reclaimed**

If Cooper represents argument prosecuted, David Brown represents symbolism punished.

Brown was a Revolutionary War veteran from Dedham, Massachusetts.[^55] In 1798, he participated in raising a liberty pole — a symbol Americans had used during the Revolution to protest British authority.[^56]

Attached to the pole was a placard condemning:

No Stamp act; no sedition; no alien bill; no land tax.

Downfall to the tyrants of America; peace and

retirement to the President; long live the vice

President and the minority; May moral government

be the basis of civil government.[^57]

The wording included language accusing the administration of tyranny and calling for political change.

Brown was arrested and prosecuted under the Sedition Act.[^58] Justice Samuel Chase again presided.[^59]

Unlike Cooper, Brown refused to provide names of collaborators. Chase reportedly pressured him to identify others involved in the liberty pole protest. Brown declined.[^60]

The sentence was severe: eighteen months in prison and a $480 fine.[^61]

It was one of the harshest penalties imposed under the Sedition Act.[^62]

Smith records that Brown’s sentence exceeded many other sedition convictions and reflected Chase’s stern approach toward what he viewed as organized defiance.[^63]

What makes Brown’s case historically striking is the symbolism. He was not accused of violence. He was punished for words displayed in a public protest — words invoking the language of the American Revolution against the federal government.

To Federalists, this resembled destabilizing radicalism — French-style disorder imported into American politics.[^64]

To Republicans, it resembled the Revolution itself — liberty language deployed against overreach.[^65]

Brown’s prosecution thus became a struggle over ownership of the founding.

Who gets to claim the Revolution’s vocabulary?

The government that enforces order?

Or the dissenters who invoke its promises?

---

# **VI. Backlash and the Election of 1800**

By 1800, enforcement of the Sedition Act had become a political liability for the Federalists.

Trials were public. Editors were jailed. Fines were heavy.[^66] The prosecutions were widely reported and deeply polarizing. As James Morton Smith observes, the Sedition Act cases “became campaign issues of the first magnitude.”[^67]

Republicans framed the Act as an assault on liberty and the First Amendment.[^68] Federalists defended it as necessary to preserve order during crisis.[^69]

The election of 1800 became, in part, a referendum on the Acts.

John Adams lost.[^70] Thomas Jefferson and the Democratic-Republicans gained control of the presidency and Congress.

Three of the four Acts did not survive the transfer of power:

* The **Sedition Act** expired by its own terms on March 3, 1801.[^71]

* The **Alien Friends Act** likewise expired in 1800.[^72]

* The **Naturalization Act of 1798** was repealed in 1802 and replaced with a shorter residency requirement.[^73]

Jefferson moved quickly to symbolically dismantle the Sedition Act’s legacy. On July 4, 1801, he issued a proclamation pardoning those convicted under it and remitting fines that had not yet been collected.[^74]

In a letter explaining his reasoning, Jefferson called the Sedition Act “a nullity” and a violation of constitutional principles.[^75]

The political verdict of 1800 was clear: three of the four emergency measures were rejected by the electorate.

But one remained.

---

# **VII. The Alien Enemies Act: The Law That Stayed**

The Alien Enemies Act differed structurally from the other three statutes.

First, it applied only during declared war.[^76]  
 Second, it contained no expiration clause.[^77]

It authorized the President, once war was declared, to detain, restrain, secure, and remove nationals of a hostile power residing in the United States.[^78]

Unlike the Alien Friends Act, it required a state of declared war. Unlike the Sedition Act, it did not regulate speech. Unlike the Naturalization Act, it did not alter political incorporation. It was framed as a wartime security measure.

Because of that framing, it endured.

---

## **The War of 1812**

During the War of 1812, the Act was invoked against British nationals residing in the United States.[^79] It established the precedent that the statute was not a dead letter of 1798, but a usable wartime authority.

---

## **World War I and the 1918 Amendment**

During World War I, President Woodrow Wilson issued proclamations under the Act targeting German nationals.[^80]

In 1918, Congress amended the statute. The amendment removed language limiting its application to “males,” thereby extending its coverage to women.[^81]

This amendment is significant for your long-shadow thesis.

It demonstrates that the statute was not merely an old relic rediscovered during crisis. It was actively maintained and modified by Congress in the twentieth century.

The relevant amendment appears in the Act of April 16, 1918, ch. 55, 40 Stat. 531.[^82]

---

## **World War II**

After the attack on Pearl Harbor, President Franklin D. Roosevelt issued Proclamation 2525 (December 7, 1941), invoking the Alien Enemies Act against Japanese nationals.[^83]

The proclamation authorized apprehension, detention, and removal of “alien enemies” where the Attorney General deemed it necessary for public safety.[^84]

Similar proclamations were issued regarding German and Italian nationals.[^85]

It is important to distinguish here — as careful historians do — that the mass internment of Japanese American citizens was carried out primarily under Executive Order 9066 and separate authorities.[^86]

However, the Alien Enemies Act formed the legal basis for detaining and regulating non-citizen nationals of enemy states during the war.[^87]

The statute remains codified today at 50 U.S.C. § 21.[^88]

---

# **VIII. Long Shadow: Constitutional and Structural Legacy**

Modern constitutional scholars continue to analyze the Alien Enemies Act as part of the broader architecture of emergency executive power.

David Cole argues that “enemy alien” status historically created a distinct category in American law, one that permitted expanded governmental authority during wartime.[^89]

Karen Nelson Moore notes that litigation involving alien enemies has shaped debates about due process and the scope of executive detention power.[^90]

The point is not that the 1798 Congress anticipated twentieth-century global war. It is that the institutional mechanism they built — a statute granting the executive detention authority during declared war — proved adaptable across centuries.

Three Acts were born in fear and died in politics.

One Act was born in fear and survived in structure.
